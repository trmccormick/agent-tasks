# GalaxyGame Visual Philosophy

This document is the foundation for all GalaxyGame visual design. It answers: **"What should this game look like?"**

Not technical specifications. Not asset lists. Just philosophy.

---

## Grounded

Technology should look **buildable**.

Not magic. Not fantasy. Real aerospace engineering—the kind that could exist.

Think NASA/ESA, not Star Trek. Think SpaceX rockets, not warp drives.

Every component should answer: "Could humans actually manufacture this?"

---

## Functional

Every visible part exists for a reason.

No decorative elements. No unnecessary details just to look cool.

- Thick walls = pressure rating
- Clamps visible = modular assembly
- Ribs = structural reinforcement
- Sealed rings = pressurized connection

**Players should read function from form.**

---

## Progressive

Players should immediately recognize tech level.

- Mk1: Improvised, thick members, visible layer lines, oversized components
- Mk2: Cleaner, fewer visible fasteners, welded joints
- Mk3: Integrated systems, polished, nearly seamless
- Mk4: Futuristic, optimized, perfectly seamless
- Mk5: Alien-elegant, impossible to see how it works

**No label needed. Shape + finish = tech level.**

---

## Modular

Everything should appear **connectable and expandable**.

Assets must visually communicate:
- "This plugs into that"
- "This can be upgraded"
- "This works with other components"

Connection points are visible. Seams are honest. Assembly is apparent.

---

## Industrial

Even advanced technology should feel **manufactured**, not magical.

- Evidence of production (tool marks, welding, casting patterns)
- Materials are honest (don't pretend plastic is metal)
- Wear is visible on older equipment
- Manufacturing origin shows (Earth-cast vs. Luna-printed vs. bootstrap-welded)

**Players see engineering, not fantasy.**

---

## Human

Objects should show evidence of **human engineering and constraints**.

- Maintenance access ports (places to work inside)
- Handholds and footholds (humans have to service this)
- Thermal vents and exhaust (equipment generates heat)
- Labels and standardized attachment points (designed for assembly)
- Visible safety systems (edge guards, containment, pressure relief)

**Not sterile. Not perfect. Human-made.**

---

## Simulation First

Visuals **communicate gameplay**, not decorate it.

- Color indicates resource type (blue = gas, brown = mineral, etc.)
- Shape indicates function (circle = gas, droplet = liquid, hex = metal)
- Texture indicates material (brushed aluminum ≠ cast concrete)
- Damage shows progression (pristine → scratched → broken → destroyed)
- Animation indicates state (idle vs. active vs. malfunction)

**If it's visible, it means something to gameplay.**

---

## Location-Agnostic

The same asset works on Luna, Mars, Venus, Titan, and beyond.

Location affects:
- Resource availability (where it's found)
- Extraction difficulty (how hard to get)
- Performance output (equipment efficiency varies)
- Formation method (what process creates it)

Location does **NOT** affect:
- The asset itself
- Visual appearance
- Material characteristics
- Fundamental design

**Same icon = same resource everywhere.**

---

## Consistency Over Innovation

A unified visual language beats novelty every time.

After 20 hours of gameplay, players should recognize:
- Every resource by color + shape, not by reading labels
- Every tech level by silhouette + finish
- Every manufacturing method by surface treatment
- Every equipment type by functional design

**Consistency is the feature.**

When creating assets, ask:
1. Is this grounded? (Looks buildable?)
2. Is this functional? (Every part has reason?)
3. Is this progressive? (Clear tech level?)
4. Is this modular? (Visibly connectable?)
5. Is this industrial? (Engineered, not magical?)
6. Is this human? (Evidence of human work?)
7. Does this communicate simulation? (Meaningful visually?)
8. Is this location-agnostic? (Works everywhere?)
9. Does this maintain consistency? (Fits visual language?)

If the answer to any question is "no," revise until yes.

---

## The Goal

Not "make cool looking assets."

**Build a system that generates thousands of consistent, functional, progression-clear, beautiful assets automatically.**

One asset defined in JSON → one blueprint entry → one prompt template → hundreds of generations → consistent game universe.

That's the philosophy in practice.
